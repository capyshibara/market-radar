# Product Academy — Wellness-linked life insurance

**A learning case for the Product Department**  
**Evidence date:** 16 July 2026  
**Purpose:** explain a product concept from public evidence. This is not a launch recommendation, pricing opinion or financial-performance claim.

## 1. The concept in one sentence

**Wellness-linked life insurance combines an insurance contract with an ongoing programme that encourages healthy behaviour and gives customers a defined reward or service when they engage.**

Traditional life insurance is mostly invisible until a claim, a review or a premium due date. A wellness-linked proposition creates a more frequent relationship: customers activate a membership, complete eligible activities, see points or status, and may receive a benefit according to stated rules.

The model is already visible in Vietnam. AIA Vietnam’s public page for **AIA – Khỏe An Nhiên** describes a healthy-living reward of up to 60% of average premiums paid in the preceding three years, subject to product conditions. [AIA – Khỏe An Nhiên](https://www.aia.com.vn/vi/san-pham/bao-ve-cuoc-song/aia-khoe-an-nhien.html)

## 2. Three things that must not be confused

| Term | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Insurance cover** | Contractual payment/service when an insured event occurs. | Death benefit; hospitalisation reimbursement; critical-illness benefit. |
| **Wellness programme** | Engagement layer providing activities, support, points, status or rewards. | Health check, step challenge, app-based coaching. |
| **Product linkage** | Rule connecting programme participation to an insurance-related reward/service. | A reward calculated from membership status and premiums paid. |

An insurer can offer wellness with no insurance linkage. It can also offer an insurance reward without wearable data. A wellness-linked product deliberately connects the two, so product terms, technology, data governance and customer communications must work together.

## 3. How it works: the customer journey

```text
Choose a qualifying product + opt into programme
                 ↓
Give informed consent and activate membership
                 ↓
Complete eligible activities through app or partner
                 ↓
Activity is validated → points/status are calculated
                 ↓
Customer sees progress, reminders and rewards
                 ↓
At the stated assessment date, eligibility rules are applied
                 ↓
Contractual reward/service is paid or made available
```

AIA Vitality Vietnam publicly describes tools, support and incentives to encourage physical activity, healthy nutrition and regular health checks; it says the programme is delivered through the Vitality Vietnam app. [AIA Vitality Vietnam FAQ](https://www.aia.com.vn/vi/song-khoe/song-khoe-cung-aia/aia-vitality/cau-hoi-thuong-gap.html)

## 4. Worked public example: a periodic wellness reward

The public product terms for AIA Vietnam’s **Bùng Gia Lực** health product show a complete rule-based design:

1. Core/optional insurance benefits are defined separately from the Vitality reward.
2. The reward is assessed on the third contract anniversary, then every three years.
3. Health cover and Vitality membership must be in force, and premiums must have been paid at the assessment date.
4. The reward is calculated as a stated reward rate × the average premium paid in the preceding three contract years.
5. The rate changes with stated Vitality membership status and in-force conditions; the reward is capped at 60%.
6. The terms say this reward does not otherwise change the insurance benefits in the core-benefits chapter.

[AIA Bùng Gia Lực product terms, pp. 8–9](https://www.aia.com.vn/content/dam/vn-wise/san-pham/suc-khoe/benh-hiem-ngheo/tron-tam-an/021025-Quy-tac-va-dieu-khoan-Bao-hiem-Suc-khoe-Bung-Gia-Luc.pdf)

This example is useful because it shows timing, formula, conditions and cap. It does **not** mean another insurer should copy the formula or can achieve the same economics.

## 5. Product components

| Component | Customer experience | What Product must define |
|---|---|---|
| **Base policy** | Familiar protection promise. | Covered events, benefit limits, premium, term, claims process, insurance exclusions. |
| **Eligibility** | Who can join and when. | Eligible products, opt-in/out, geography, member status. |
| **Activities** | Actions that can earn progress. | Permitted actions, validation, points rules, annual limits, missing-data treatment. |
| **Status/reward** | A visible level, reward or service. | Thresholds, assessment date, cap, payment method, expiry, change-control process. |
| **Digital journey** | App, devices, reminders, help. | Authentication, accessibility, partner integration, outages and service recovery. |
| **Data/consent** | Clear explanation of data use. | Data minimisation, consent, vendor access, retention, security and complaint process. |
| **Governance** | Consistent decisions. | Product ownership, actuarial approval, privacy/compliance sign-off and audit trail. |

## 6. The operating model

This is not “an app added by Marketing.” It has six operational owners:

| Owner | Responsibility |
|---|---|
| **Product / Actuarial** | Benefit design, caps, reward funding, product wording, renewal and monitoring. |
| **Digital / Technology** | App journey, identity, interfaces, availability, data quality and fraud signals. |
| **Wellness partner** | Activities, devices, coaching, rewards or health checks under a controlled service agreement. |
| **Operations / Customer Service** | Enrolment questions, status disputes, reward corrections, payments and complaints. |
| **Risk / Compliance / Privacy** | Consent, disclosures, fairness, outsourcing, security and regulatory approval. |
| **Claims / Underwriting** | Maintains the approved boundary with wellness data. |

The public AIA FAQ distinguishes the wellness-programme operator from the life insurer and says basic screening results are not used to underwrite existing policies or handle claims. This is a useful design principle: engagement data and claims/underwriting decisions should not be assumed to be interchangeable. [AIA Vitality Vietnam FAQ](https://www.aia.com.vn/vi/song-khoe/song-khoe-cung-aia/aia-vitality/cau-hoi-thuong-gap.html)

## 7. Benefits

### Customer value

- A reason to engage between purchase and claim.
- Support, reminders and rewards for defined healthy activities.
- A more tangible proposition than protection alone.
- In a linked design, a rule-based benefit customers can monitor.

### Insurer value

- More frequent, permissioned interaction.
- A differentiated protection proposition.
- A platform for prevention, healthcare-navigation or employee-benefit partners.
- Participation data, which is **not automatically** proof of improved health, risk or claims economics.

### International design examples

AIA Group describes a Vitality-based employee offer with rewards, health insight and support. John Hancock has publicly described a diabetes-focused life-insurance solution paired with coaching, clinical support, education and rewards. These are examples of possible mechanisms, not proof of universal commercial value. [AIA Group Live Well](https://www.aia.com/en/about-aia/solutions-to-meet-your-needs/regional-solutions/products-services/live-well) [John Hancock Aspire](https://www.manulife.com/en/news/john-hancock-verily-onduo-launch-aspire-life-insurance-for-americans-with-diabetes.html)

## 8. Exclusions, limits and conditions

There are **two different categories** of limitation.

### A. Insurance exclusions and limits

These are normal policy rules: covered events, waiting periods, benefit sub-limits, geographic scope, renewal, non-payment, claims evidence and exclusions. They must be read from the actual policy wording. A wellness reward does not remove those rules.

For example, the Bùng Gia Lực terms include waiting periods and continuation/renewal conditions. [AIA Bùng Gia Lực product terms](https://www.aia.com.vn/content/dam/vn-wise/san-pham/suc-khoe/benh-hiem-ngheo/tron-tam-an/021025-Quy-tac-va-dieu-khoan-Bao-hiem-Suc-khoe-Bung-Gia-Luc.pdf)

### B. Wellness-programme eligibility limits

These decide whether a customer earns a reward or retains a programme status. Typical limits include:

- membership and qualifying policy must remain active;
- premiums must be paid;
- only defined activities/partners/devices count;
- rewards have a cap and a specified assessment date;
- participation may not suit every customer situation or product version;
- the programme can have separate fees, cancellation rules or terms.

**Product rule:** never call a wellness reward “guaranteed savings” without its conditions, timing and cap. Never imply that an activity changes underwriting, claims eligibility or medical advice unless approved product rules explicitly say so.

## 9. Economics, fairness and evidence

A wellness programme is a cost commitment: reward funding, partner/device cost, technology, customer service, fraud/error remediation and take-up all matter. Product also needs to test fairness: a reward structure should not unintentionally exclude customers because of disability, age, location, device access or health circumstances.

Engagement does not itself prove lower mortality, morbidity, claims cost or higher retention. Swiss Re’s March 2026 work on Singapore’s LumiHealth programme focuses on quantifying mortality impact. That is the correct standard: define an evaluation design before making a health or financial-outcome claim. [Swiss Re: digital health and mortality](https://www.swissre.com/reinsurance/insights/digital-health-programme-impact-on-mortality.html)

## 10. A prudent discovery pilot

Start with a **service-and-engagement pilot**, not a premium-discount or underwriting change.

1. Choose one customer group and one simple, accessible activity.
2. Make participation voluntary; explain data use in plain language.
3. Offer a fixed, capped non-insurance reward/service.
4. Keep wellness data out of claims and underwriting unless an approved rule says otherwise.
5. Measure enrolment, sustained engagement, complaints, cost per active customer and customer understanding.
6. Decide only after the pilot whether stronger product linkage is justified.

## 11. Product-team study questions

1. Which problem are we solving: engagement, prevention, relevance, employer value or underwriting access?
2. What exactly earns a reward, and can a customer verify it?
3. What can the customer lose by not participating? Is that fair and clearly disclosed?
4. Who resolves incorrect device data or a status dispute?
5. What data is essential—and what data should we deliberately not use?
6. What evidence is sufficient before claiming a health, risk or financial outcome?
